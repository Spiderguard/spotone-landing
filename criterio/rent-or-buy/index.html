Rent or buy? The answer is a number, not a dogma.

"Renting is throwing money away" sounds good. The real answer depends on how long you stay — and what you do with the difference.

"Renting is throwing money away."

You hear it all the time.

Sometimes it's true.

Often it's not.

Buying and renting aren't good and bad. They're two different calculations.

And the answer depends on one variable almost no one runs first:

How long are you really going to stay?

Because buying has costs that don't come back.

Closing.
Transfer tax (ITBI).
Moving.
Maintenance.
The time and cost of selling later.

If you leave fast, those numbers weigh more than they seem.

So someone who moves in two or three years can end up better off renting. Someone who stays longer may find more sense in buying.

It depends.

And here's a part of the conversation that rarely comes up:

What do you do with the difference?

Because buying also ties up capital. The down payment, closing costs, and liquidity you no longer have available.

If you rent and that difference simply disappears, it's one calculation. If you invest it, save it, or use it to build something, the calculation changes.

The question isn't "is renting throwing money away?". It's "how long am I going to stay… and what do I do with the difference?".

What I'd confirm before deciding:
— How many years you really plan to stay.
— The full real cost of buying (mortgage + HOA + insurance + tax + maintenance).
— The real cost of renting something comparable.
— What you'd do with the money you don't tie up if you rent.

Buying doesn't always win. Renting doesn't either.

The one who wins is the one who runs the numbers before deciding.
