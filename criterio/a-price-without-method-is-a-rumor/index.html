A price without a method is just a rumor.

When someone asks "how much can I sell for?", the serious answer is rarely an exact number — it's a defensible range, and the reasoning behind it.

When someone asks me "how much can I sell for?" —or "what's it really worth, and why?"— the most serious answer is almost never an exact number.

It's a defensible range. And the "why" behind it.

In Panama there's no public MLS with closed prices: what something truly sold for is rarely visible. A price thrown out without a method isn't a price — it's a rumor with a number attached.

This is how I work it:

First, I ask for the minimum to avoid guessing.
Area, building, size, bedrooms and baths, floor or unit format, condition, HOA fee, real maintenance costs, and your target price if you have one. Without that, any number is noise.

Then I answer in a range, not a guess.
"Based on what we have, the defensible range is between X and Y, because…". That "because" is what separates judgment from gut feeling. And the range widens or tightens depending on how defensible the references are (appraisal, referenced closes, real costs, selling timelines).

The "why" comes in three layers — the same three I run when buying:

— Price per m² against defensible area references: appraisal, referenced closes, adjustments. Asking is not selling.
— Real monthly cost: mortgage, HOA, insurance, tax, maintenance, plus a buffer for surprises.
— If it's an investment, rent vs cost: annual rent over price minus vacancy and those costs. Brochure yield is the brochure's yield.

And I mark the risk, so I don't overpromise.
If we can't confirm what closed nearby, precision drops. Even then, you can define an honest range by triangulating appraisal and references.

An exact number sounds safe. A method-backed range is safe.

What I would confirm before pricing:
— What actually closed nearby (not asking prices).
— The full real monthly cost.
— Today's demand and selling timelines for the area.
